A1- Design

By Sopheak Neak

After the World War II, antibiotics were considered as "wonder drugs", since they were an easy remedy for what had been intractable ailments. To learn which drug worked most effectively for which bacterial infection, performance of the three most popular antibiotics on 16 bacteria were gathered.

The values in the dataset represent the minimum inhibitory concentration (MIC), a measure of the effectiveness of the antibiotic, which represents the concentration of antibiotic required to prevent growth in vitro. The reaction of the bacteria to Gram staining is described by the covariate “gram staining”. Bacteria that are stained dark blue or violet are Gram-positive. Otherwise, they are Gram-negative.

Visualization #1

Which antibiotic is the most effective (has the lowest MIC) in preventing growth in vitro for differing bacterial infections?

The dataset has an abundant data points that varied in range and sign, however, in order to capture a great multitude of data points, I’ve decided to utilize a dot plot for graphical encoding. The main visual decoding task, in term of amplifying understanding of the data, for this graph is comparing the value of Log MIC for each bacteria between the differing antibiotics.

The most accurate elementary perceptual task is position along a common scale. A dot graph does a great job at easing the task of comparing values. Readers can easily distinguish if differing antibiotics have the same Log MIC by looking checking whether the dots are overlapping one another. In addition, the differing color of the dots give a good contrast, which helps reader easily distinguish which dot is of higher or lower value.

I’ve decided to connect the lines between each dot in order to give users a better sense of separation between each bacteria. If the dots are not connected, readers will have a harder time comparing the high and low Log MIC value of antibiotics for each bacteria at a quick glance. Also, to my defense, the line does not add or introduce any out-of-scope data, because the value on the x axis is nominal. Hence, reader cannot make assumption or establish a non-introduced trend from looking at the lines.

Visualization #2

Which antibiotic is more effective (has the lowest MIC) on gram-positive bacteria and gram-negative bacteria?

The main visual decoding task for this graph is comparing the distributing of value for antibiotics in gram-positive and in gram-negative bacteria; a box plot is a great graphical encoding tool to do so. A box plot utilizes the most accurate elementary perceptual task, which is comparing the position along the common scale. Readers can easily distinguish the difference of low, high, median, and IQR for each antibiotic. Because each antibiotics aligns next to one another, it enhances the accuracy of comparing the value and also gives readers a greater understanding of the antibiotic’s effective on differing type of gram staining.

The design also keeps readers’ attention focus on one demographic of the data set. By providing a large difference in spacing for gram-positive and gram-negative bacteria, readers unconsciously comparing only the values in each gram stain. The sense of separation helps making the data more digestible. I also did not fully fill in the marker of the box, because I’d like provide readers the ability to see the graph value line as a mean to comparing the difference in position between antibiotics data point. In addition, I keep the color consistency of the antibiotics between the dot plot and this graph; this ensures that readers would not need to learn additional information on the data.

Visualization #3

On average, how do antibiotics perform on differing gram stain bacteria (gram-positive and gram-negative)?

The main visual decoding task for this bar graph is comparing the average Log MIC between gram-positive and gram-negative bacteria between differing antibiotics. Luckily, the visual decoding task can easily be graphical encoded using the most accurate elementary perceptual task: position along a common scale. A grouped bar graph allows reader to easily comparing values between one another. Therefore, to compare average Log MIC between gram-positive and gram-negative, readers can simply compare how each bar stands, taller or shorter, next to one another.

In addition, since the main visual decoding task consists of only comparing gram-positive to gram-negative, the graph;s design has the advantage in accomplishing the elementary perceptual task of comparing position along a common scale. Because there are only two bars for each antibiotic, the bars are guaranteed to stand next to each other. This enhances the ability for readers to comparing the difference regardless of the threshold of difference; whether the threshold of difference is high or low, the fact that it stands next to each other, it increases the accuracy of distinguishing the position of each value on the graph.
